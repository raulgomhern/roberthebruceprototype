Robert the Bruce (1274–1329)

Robert the Bruce (1274–1329) was King of Scotland from 1306 and is regarded as one of the nation’s greatest heroes for leading the fight for independence against England.

He died in 1329 at Cardross and was buried at Dunfermline Abbey.

As his last wish, he requested that his heart be taken to the Holy Land, but after a journey that reached only as far as Spain, it was returned to Scotland and interred at Melrose Abbey.

After King Robert I died in 1329, he was buried in the choir of Dunfermline Abbey, where a monument imported from Paris—commissioned before his death—originally marked his grave but was later lost, likely destroyed after the Reformation.

Archaeological findings

In 1818, during construction work at Dunfermline Abbey, workers accidentally discovered a shallow tomb beneath two large stone slabs containing a skeleton in linen cloth interwoven with threads of gold, over the head was lead crown, that, based on matches with medieval chronicles and due to other royal burials nearby, was identified as the remains of Robert the Bruce.

Excavations from the late 18th to early 19th centuries uncovered fragments of carved stone thought to be from Bruce’s tomb monument; these are now held in Scottish museum collections but had not been comprehensively studied together until recently.

Separately, a small conical casket was discovered beneath Melrose Abbey’s Chapter House floor in 1921, quietly reburied, and rediscovered in 1996. Although it remains unconfirmed, this casket is believed to have once held Robert the Bruce’s heart. In 1998, it was reinterred beneath a memorial stone dedicated to him.

Anatomical reports

In 1819, the first documented study of the skull was conducted by the phrenologist George Combe and the surgeon Robert Liston, who noted that the skull was “in perfect condition,” except it showed some damage to the left eye socket and cheek bone and some missing teeths, possibly from injuries received in battle.

They thus rejected the idea that the king suffered from a disease such as leprosy.

In this context, the artist William Scoular made a plaster cast of the skull. Several copies of this cast exist, housed for example at Dunfermline Abbey, the Hunterian Museum at the University of Glasgow, and the Anatomical Museum of the University of Edinburgh.

After examination, the skeleton was reburied, sealed with pitch, although reports indicate some bone fragments and relics were stolen. Since the original remains remain sealed, subsequent studies have relied exclusively on the skull casts.

These molds have been essential both for historical analysis and for the phrenological studies of the time.

Phrenology was a popular discipline at that time that aimed to
deduce mental and personality traits from the shape of the skull.

In these models you can explore the different elements studied by the phrenologists.

In 1832, William Scott’s phrenological study of Robert the Bruce’s skull cast highlighted several cranial areas according to nineteenth-century phrenology.

He pointed to strong development in regions associated with firmness and destructiveness (interpreted as willpower and determination), as well as areas linked to leadership and ambition.

Scott also noted the prominence of zones thought to reflect intellect and cautious planning, relating these features to Bruce’s strategic mind and historical reputation for courage and leadership.

These molds also served to create freehand and artistic facial reconstructions inspired by these previous phrenological studies.

This comparison shows two interpretations of King Robert the Bruce: a traditional 1966 reconstruction and a modern approach using updated anatomical data.

Currently, based on the scientific study of the skull and using precise techniques, a team from the University of Glasgow and the University of Stirling, together with Historic Environment Scotland, has produced a plausible facial reconstruction.
